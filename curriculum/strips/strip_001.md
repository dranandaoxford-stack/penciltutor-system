# Strip 001 — Observation vs Assumption

## Scientific Reasoning Rule
Do not confuse what you observe with what you assume it means.

---

## Learning Objective (Ages 10–14)
Students learn to separate direct observation from interpretation and understand why science begins with what is actually seen, measured, or recorded.

---

## Characters
- **Alex** — curious, jumps to conclusions
- **Sam** — careful thinker, asks clarifying questions

---

## Panel Script

### Panel 1
**Visual:** A wilted plant sitting by a sunny classroom window.  
**Alex:** “The plant is dying!”

---

### Panel 2
**Visual:** Sam kneels closer, looking carefully at the plant.  
**Sam:** “What do we actually see?”

---

### Panel 3
**Visual:** Close-up of dry soil and drooping leaves.  
**Alex:** “The leaves are droopy… and the soil looks dry.”

---

### Panel 4
**Visual:** Sam holding a watering can.  
**Sam:** “That’s an observation. ‘Dying’ is an assumption.”

---

### Panel 5
**Visual:** The plant after watering, leaves starting to perk up.  
**Alex:** “Oh… it wasn’t dying. It was thirsty.”

---

### Panel 6
**Visual:** Both students smiling, plant healthy.  
**Caption:** In science, start with observations before making conclusions.

---

## Scientific Takeaway
Observations are facts you can see or measure.  
Assumptions are explanations — and they must be tested.
